The Musical Thread How Laurie Scheepers writes music — and what runs through all 78 tracks

Not the lyrics — the music. Four frontier models read the same measured data (librosa-extracted tempo and key, plus energy and darkness scores) for every track across seven movements, and were asked the same question: what makes it all recognisably him?

The headline, in one line

All four models, reasoning independently, landed on the same single signature: he writes in E.

E major (13) and E minor (11) together account for 24 of 78 tracks (~31%) — the next most common key, D major, has only 8. Roughly three times the rate of anything else. Not a coincidence of taste: the shape of a hand on a guitar neck, and it survives every genre swerve he takes.

E is the most resonant key on a standard-tuned guitar — the open low and high E strings ring through almost any voicing, and the I–IV–V in E (E–A–B) sits under the fingers in open position. When a catalogue clusters this hard on E, the simplest explanation is guitar-first writing with open-position muscle memory: the key is chosen by the hand before the ear gets a vote. The thread holds across wildly different genres — Golden Hair (E major, acoustic folk), Ek is die een (E major, alt-rock), Fuck Everything (E minor, the darkest track at 0.9), the river must flow (E major, classical-guitar instrumental). Four genres, one root note.

Where the council disagreed — resolved against the data OpenAI and xAI claimed the major/minor split "maps cleanly onto the movements." DeepSeek refuted it, and the data agrees: E appears in six of seven movements, and The Confessionals alone holds both — five E-major tracks and three E-minor. The honest, stronger claim: the E root is the constant; major-versus-minor is a local mood choice, not a movement boundary. He reaches for the same shape, then decides song by song whether it is bright or grieving.

Falsified if: future work centres on a non-guitar key (B-flat, F) or abandons E as the modal home — that would make the E-centre a phase, not a method.

02

Tempo & rhythm — the 123 cluster and the 172 illusion

Tempo across 78 tracks: min 86, max 172, mean 124, median 123. The catalogue lives in a tight ~99–143 BPM band. 123 BPM is close to a natural moderate strum-and-sing pace — a default body-tempo. He does not write to a click; he writes to a pulse, and it is roughly the same pulse most weeks.

DeepSeek's catch — the strongest tempo insight The 172 BPM ceiling (You've got to hide your love away, There's More to All of This, Jam, Sometimes, the Workshop mixes) is almost exactly double 86 and close to double the median. These are very likely felt in half-time — the underlying pulse is ~86 and librosa is counting the faster subdivision. The proof: Jam sustains 172 BPM over a 71-minute take. Nobody plays fast for 71 minutes. So the experienced tempo range is narrower than the numbers imply; the cluster is even tighter than it looks.

The 86 floor (Luister, his only sub-89 track) is a deliberate drag — and a demo, fitting a writer who slows right down when it is just him and the guitar. The genuine accelerations are the full-band pushes: Drink tequila uit 'n skoen (152 BPM, energy 0.8) and Sometimes (152 BPM, prog-metal). Speed arrives with the band, not in the writing.

Falsified if: a track lands genuinely above ~200 or below ~80 BPM at its felt pulse, or the 172 tracks prove truly double-time in performance rather than half-time hypermeter.

The outliers define the boundaries of his world rather than betraying drift. Maximum intensity: Fuck Everything (E0.8 / D0.9) and Trip to the Circus (E0.9 / D0.8) — the top-right corner. Maximum levity: howzit my bru (E0.65 / D0.15) — the single lightest track, proof he can write bright, he just rarely chooses to. The striking fact: the same writer who makes E0.2 confessionals also makes E0.9 noise pieces, and both stay anchored near darkness 0.5. The energy dial swings hugely; the darkness dial barely moves off centre. That is a temperament — a melancholy that is constant regardless of volume.

Falsified if: measured more precisely, darkness spreads as widely as energy — that would undercut "constant darkness, variable energy."

04

The thread — what makes it all recognisably him

The central question, and the council answer is unusually unanimous: the unifying signature is harmonic, not melodic or dynamic. It is the E-centre, reinforced by the midline darkness. Stated honestly: from measured metadata you can defend a harmonic habit (key distribution) and a temperamental constant (darkness clustered near 0.5 across every movement). You cannot directly defend a "melodic instinct" or a dynamic "swerve" from tempo/key/energy alone — those live in the notes and the waveform. xAI was explicit and correct: "melodic or dynamic swerve is not measurable here; harmonic habit is." So the defensible thread is the one the numbers carry:

He returns to E, and he returns to the middle of the darkness axis — no matter the genre. The root note and the emotional weight stay put while everything else (tempo feel, energy, instrumentation, language) changes around them.

That is a real, falsifiable signature: the same hand and the same mood underneath deliberately different clothes. The breadth of genre is the performance; the constancy of E and of mid-darkness is the person doing it.

A hypothesis, not a finding The lyrical "swerve" — set up, then undercut — has a plausible musical cousin in the energy/darkness split: songs that are energetic and dark (Nemesis E0.6/D0.7, Opportunities… E0.7/D0.75, Sometimes E0.8/D0.7) — a bright surface over a heavy centre. Offered as a hypothesis only; it would need an audio pass to confirm.

The demo-to-full-band families are the key evidence. Three songs exist in multiple versions, and what changes — and what doesn't — is revealing.

Key and tempo stabilise at the demo stage and barely move thereafter. Energy and darkness are the production variables — what arrangement and mixing actually change.

That is exactly the profile of a guitarist who demos a song in its final key (usually E), at its final pulse, alone — and then layers the band, the distortion, the space on top without touching the harmonic or rhythmic skeleton. The song is written on the guitar; it is arranged in the room, and you can see the seam between the two stages in the numbers. The seven "movements" reinforce this: they are arrangement-and-genre categories (voice+guitar / full band / instrumental / drone / covers / workshop / Afrikaans), not harmonic ones — which is why E bleeds across six of them. The movements describe how a song was dressed, not how it was conceived.

Limits — what this can and cannot claim

  • Inferred from measured metadata plus four models' reading — not from re-listening to 78 waveforms. Key, tempo, energy and darkness are summary numbers; they do not contain the actual notes, chord progressions, melodic contours, or production detail.
  • Tempo carries a known artefact. librosa is prone to octave errors (half- or double-time). The 172 BPM ceiling is very likely a half-time mis-count of an ~86 pulse — treat the measured range as wider than the felt range.
  • Energy and darkness are model-assigned 0–1 scores, not physical units. Consistent enough to compare tracks against each other, but the absolute values (and the darkness midline) are a measurement convention, not ground truth.
  • The strongest unfalsifiable-from-this-data claims were deliberately withheld. "Melodic instinct" and the musical "swerve" live in the audio, not the metadata — flagged as hypotheses, not findings.

What a deeper audio-DSP pass would add: chord-progression extraction (does a recurring I–IV–V reinforce the E-centre?), melodic-contour analysis (a genuine melodic signature?), beat-tracking with octave-error correction (settle the 172 question), spectral/timbre analysis (the actual sound of his guitar and voice, the grain of The Dark Room), and structural dynamics across each song's timeline (test the "swerve" — does the music set up and undercut, section by section?). Those would turn the defensible harmonic fingerprint here into a full musical portrait.

Four models, one conclusion — openly disagreed and resolved where they differed. The thread is E, and the middle of the dark.
